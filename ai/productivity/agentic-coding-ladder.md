# The Agentic Coding Ladder is a List of Things You Give Up

**Source:** https://jedarden.com/notes/agentic-coding-ladder/
**Saved:** 2026-06-03
**Tags:** ai, productivity, learning, craft, agentic-ai, prompting

---

## TL;DR
A complement to Yegge's 8-level AI trust framework, measuring the same ascent by what you renounce at each rung. The climb has two halves and two thresholds: first you stop writing code (rungs 1-5), then you give up your presence (rungs 6-9). Seniority is friction — experience means more habits to unlearn. And the skill at the top isn't living high; it's choosing the right rung per task.

## Key Concepts & Terms
- **Threshold 1 — you stop writing code**: Between rungs 5 and 6. No smaller unit left to hand off; the axis flips from "what you delegate" to "how present you are."
- **Threshold 2 — the conversation becomes a dispatch**: The crossing between rung 7 and 8. Agent goes headless; it can no longer reach you, can no longer ask the clarifying question that would have saved it. Every discipline at the top of the ladder exists because you removed yourself as the answer.
- **Seniority is friction**: Every rung requires giving up something experienced developers formed as identity — reading every diff, owning the function, knowing how internals work. Newcomers have nothing to unlearn and can start above the lower rungs. But skipping isn't earning — the veteran's judgment came from doing the thing they're now surrendering.
- **Break-even per rung**: The overhead of each rung (specification, planning, scaffolding, verification) only pays off above a certain task size. A cattle fleet for a one-line typo fix is chartering a freight train to mail a letter.
- **Band, not a rung**: The honest self-assessment is a band — the ceiling you can reach and the floor you actually reside on. Most people drop to rung 3 daily even if they can reach rung 9, because most work doesn't clear rung 9's break-even.

## Main Arguments & Takeaways
- **Nine rungs and their renunciations**: (1) Student — gave up Google/Stack Overflow; (2) Borrower — gave up writing boilerplate; (3) Reviewer — gave up being the author; (4) Delegator — gave up reading every line; (5) Director — gave up knowing how your own code works inside; (6) Juggler — gave up undivided focus; (7) Bottleneck — gave up the belief that attention scales; (8) Dispatcher — gave up being reachable; (9) Operator — gave up the option to inspect any individual run.
- **Rung 7 is a wall, not a capability**: The day you realize you are the slowest component in your own system. Adding sessions doesn't add throughput — it adds context-switching cost. Everyone who runs agents seriously remembers hitting this.
- **The disciplines at rung 8+ are prosthetics**: Anchoring on facts the model can't see, not letting the agent grade its own homework, making unit economics work unattended — none are optional above threshold 2. They replace the human that was just amputated from the loop.
- **The correct skill at the top is choosing the lowest rung that clears the task**: Not living as high as possible. Operating below your ceiling isn't regression — it's fit.
- **Rung 10 is fog**: Whatever still feels load-bearing at rung 9 — the thing you'd argue hardest about giving up. From inside any rung, the next surrender is invisible because you're standing on the thing you're about to give up. The real level 10 is something that, standing at 9, still feels indispensable.

## Notable Quotes
> "Seniority is friction. The more experience you have, the more attached you are to exactly the things each rung requires you to drop."

> "The most advanced thing you can do with all nine rungs available to you is pick the cheapest one that still clears the job."

> "It opens with giving up the search bar, and it ends — if it ends — with giving up something I'm not yet wise enough to miss."

## Questions & Gaps
- The break-even for each rung — is there empirical data on what task size/complexity justifies each level of overhead?
- Newcomers who skip lower rungs may lack judgment — is there a way to develop that judgment without going back to writing code manually?
- Rung 10 speculation: is "human defines what 'good' means" the last irreducible thing, or is that also delegatable?
