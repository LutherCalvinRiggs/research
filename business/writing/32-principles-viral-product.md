# 32 Principles of a Viral Product

**Source:** https://x.com/marclou/status/2063992615721435154 (pasted content by @marclou / Marc Lou)
**Saved:** 2026-06-11
**Tags:** business, writing, design, learning, career, startup

> Author context: Marc Lou — 5 years building 35 startups in public, watching hundreds of launches make $0 and a few reach millions of users. "These are not rules. They're patterns. Use them as a compass, not a checklist."

---

## TL;DR
32 distilled product and landing page principles from 35 public startup builds. The through-lines: eliminate everything that creates friction or dilutes attention, make the value instantly legible, use specificity over generality, charge more than you think you should, and build around a clear single outcome rather than a feature list. Patterns, not rules.

## Key Concepts & Terms
- **Popcorn Pricing**: Three tiers only — Good, Better, Best. Every additional pricing tier creates another decision and another reason to leave. Visitors came to buy, not study a spreadsheet.
- **OG image as YouTube thumbnail**: The Open Graph image (shown in social shares, Slack previews, iMessage links) is often seen more than the actual website. Design it to generate clicks, not to describe the product accurately.
- **Hard paywall**: Ask for payment before asking for data. Signups don't validate the product. Credit card pull = validation.
- **Hero section as the whole pitch**: 80% of visitors won't scroll past the hero. The product must be understood and wanted within seconds, before any scrolling.

## The 32 Principles (Grouped by Theme)

### Attention and Focus
- **2.** Three colors max — black text, white background, one color for the buy button. Every additional color dilutes attention.
- **6.** One idea per screen. One screen. One message. Like the Instagram feed.
- **11.** Do one thing. People don't remember Swiss Army knives. Be known for the tool that solved the specific problem.
- **22.** One call to action. Every extra button creates hesitation. Multiple paths → many choose none.

### Copy and Messaging
- **3.** Numbers over adjectives. "Fast" is forgettable. "Save 4 hours every week" is not.
- **7.** Headline a fifth grader can understand. Complexity kills curiosity.
- **9.** Copy only you could write. If a competitor could paste your landing page onto their site, it's too generic. Write from experience.
- **14.** Steal copy from customers. Customers already describe your product better than you do. Write like they talk.
- **17.** Headline people remember the next day. Write five. Show to friends. Wait 24 hours. Keep the one that sticks.
- **18.** Emotional headline. People remember feelings, not features. Make them laugh, say wow, or think "what the fuck is this."
- **21.** Show empathy before selling. Describe the problem better than the customer can. Trust comes before the solution.
- **26.** No weak words. "Most," "many," "rarely" weaken claims because nobody knows what they mean. Make statements, not estimates.
- **28.** CTA that says what happens next. "Get Started" means nothing. "Analyze My Website" tells people what they're about to do.
- **30.** Under 10 words to describe the product. If you can't, your users won't be able to either.

### Pricing
- **1.** No free plan. Less than 3% of free users convert. Free users increase support costs and pull roadmap toward features paying customers don't want.
- **8.** Hard paywall. Payment before data. No credit card pull = no validation.
- **12.** Popcorn Pricing. Three tiers: Good, Better, Best. No more.
- **16.** Make pricing impossible to miss. Put "Pricing" in the header. Visitors use the pricing section to understand the product, not just the price.
- **27.** No subscription if avoidable. One-time payments are 10× easier to sell. Don't add another monthly charge unless you genuinely can't ship without it.
- **32.** Be more expensive than competitors. Nobody talks about the second cheapest option.

### Product and Demo
- **5.** OG image as YouTube thumbnail. "If they don't click, they don't watch."
- **10.** Show the product before explaining it. A demo communicates more than paragraphs.
- **19.** Do something people have never seen before. Nobody shares another clone.
- **20.** Sellable from the hero section alone. 80% won't scroll. Fix the hero first.
- **24.** Sell a human desire, not a feature. People buy more money, more time, better health, more status, or less pain. Features are vehicles.
- **25.** Let people try before buying. Put best features on the landing page. Let people play before they pay.
- **29.** No launch without testimonials. A page without them asks strangers to trust blindly. Get users first.
- **31.** Compare to competitors. Show a simple table with the features customers actually care about. Make the decision obvious.

### Brand and Distribution
- **4.** Footer people want to share. 97% won't buy but they might share. People remember what they see last.
- **13.** Ride a wave. Build around trends and problems already being discussed. The wave does half the marketing.
- **15.** Founder people can see and hear. People buy from people. Screen recording from the founder beats a corporate promo video.
- **23.** Memorable name. Use words people already know. Avoid wordplay, made-up words, names that need explanation.

## The Tensions Worth Noting

Several principles pull against each other in practice:

- **#1** (no free plan) vs. **#25** (let people try before buying) — Marc resolves this by putting demos on the landing page rather than offering free accounts. The product is experienced without a signup.
- **#8** (hard paywall) vs. **#25** (try before buying) — same resolution: the landing page itself is the demo, not a trial account.
- **#27** (avoid subscriptions) vs. **#32** (charge more than competitors) — these can align if the one-time price is premium, but most SaaS products can't ship without recurring revenue at any meaningful scale.

## Questions & Gaps
- The "less than 3% of free users convert" figure — is this a documented cross-industry average or Marc's personal observation across 35 products? The threshold matters: some categories (B2B SaaS, developer tools) have meaningfully different conversion dynamics.
- #32 (charge more) and #27 (no subscription) pull in opposite directions for products where the value proposition is monthly recurring use. What's the resolution for a product that genuinely needs recurring revenue?
- The "hero section only" principle (#20) works for simple products. For MAC.BID's complexity (auctions, bidding, payments, shipping), how do you compress a multi-dimensional product into a single-screen pitch?
- No mention of SEO, content marketing, or community-led growth — Marc's frame is entirely landing page and launch-day virality. Does this reflect a deliberate scope limitation or a blind spot?

## Most Directly Applicable to MAC.BID

- **#3** (numbers over adjectives) — "Discounted overstock" is generic. "Products up to 90% off retail, new arrivals daily" is specific.
- **#9** (copy only you could write) — the auction mechanics, the bidding tension, the overstock sourcing story are uniquely yours. Write from that experience.
- **#20** (sellable from hero alone) — the hero needs to communicate: what it is, why the price is real, and what to do next — all before scrolling.
- **#21** (empathy before selling) — the problem MAC.BID solves is paying retail for things that exist at discount. Name that frustration before explaining the solution.
- **#28** (CTA says what happens next) — "Start Bidding" or "See Today's Deals" beats "Get Started."
